Activation_energy

The activation energy is the height of the potential barrier separating a nucleus in its ground state and the state where it is split in two daughter nuclei.

Anti-baryon

An anti-baryon is the anti-particle of a baryon. There are a bound state of three anti-quarks.

Atomic_mass

The atomic mass is the mass of a given atom in its neutral state, including the electrons.

Atomic_mass_unit

The atomic mass unit is defined as one twelveth of the mass of a \({}^{12}C\) atom

Baryon

A baryon is a bound state of three quarks. Anti-baryons are bound states of three anti-quarks. There are two types of baryons, the spin \(3/2\) baryons and the spin \(1/2\) baryons.

Boson

Bosons are particles that follow Bose statistics, meaning that any number of bosons with the same quantum numbers can occupy the same state, as opposed to fermions. Bosons have integer spins.

Decuplet

A decuplet is a multiplet with ten elements. An example is the \(SU(3)\) flavour decuplet of spin \(3/2\) baryons.

Doublet

A doublet is a multiplet with two elements. A spin \(1/2\) pair \(\uparrow\rangle\), \(|downarrow\rangle\) is an example of a doublet. For the isospin symmetry the \(u\) and \(d\) quarks build a doublet, and so do the proton and neutron.

Electron

The electron is the lightest lepton.

Fermion

Fermions are particles that satisfy the Pauli exclusion principle meaning that no two fermions with the same quantum numbers can occupy the same state, as opposed to bosons. Fermions have half-integer spins.

Flavour

The quarks come in 6 different types, the \(u\), \(d\), \(s\), \(c\), \(b\) and \(t\) quarks. These types are called flavours.

Hadron

Hadrons are particles made of quarks. This means mesons, baryons and anti-baryons.

Half_life

The half life is the time it takes for half of the particles or nuclei in a sample to decay. It is related to the lifetime through \(t_{1/2}=\ln(2)\tau\)

Isobar

Isobars are nuclei with the same mass number \(A\).

Isospin

The isospin \(I\) is a vector quantity conserved by the strong force. It is the conserved quantity related with a symmetry between \(u\) and \(d\) quark. This symmetry is exact for the strong force but only approximative when other forces are considered. Since the strong force dominates in many effects isospin conservation is often a good guide for effects of the strong force. Isospin multiplets have similar properties. As for the usual spin only one component can be determined simultaneously, we choose that component to be the third one. \(u\) quarks have \(I_3=1/2\) and \(I_3=-1/2\) for the \(d\) quark.

Isotone

Isotones are nuclei with the same number of neutrons. They have the same value of \(A-Z\).

Isotope

Isotopes are nuclei with the same number of protons (and as a consequence they have the same chemical properties.) They have the same value of \(Z\).

Lepton

Leptons are fermions with no color charge. There are two types of tleptons: the charged leptons: electron, muon, tau and their anti-particles, and the neutral leptons the electron-, muon- and tau-neutrinos and their anti-particle.

Lifetime

The lifetime is the mean time it takes a particle or nucleus to decay.

Mass_number

The mass number of a nucleus is the sum of the number of protons and the number of neutrons. It is a rough estimate of the nucleus mass in atomic mass units.

Meson

Mesons are bound states of a quark and an anti-quark. Their parity is \(-1\). They can be organised in \(SU(3)\) multiplets.

Moderator

A moderator is a medium used in a nuclear reactor to slow down neutrons to an energy where fission of aditional nuclei is the most likely process.

Multiplet

A multiplet is a group of particles that transform into each other under a symmetry transformation.

Muon

The muon is a lepton with the same quantum numbers as the electron but much heavier. One if its key characteristic is that at relativistic speeds it can penetrate matter easily.

Neutrino

Neutrinos are electrically neutral leptons. They exist in three flavours, one for each charged lepton (electron,muon and tau)

Nuclear_mass

The nuclear mass is the mass of a given nucleus, not including the electrons.

Nucleon

Nucleons is used to refer to a neutron or a proton. THe number of nucleons in a nucleus is called the mass number.

Octet

An octet is a multiplet with eight elements. One example is the octet of meson within the \(SU(3)\) flavour symmetry.

Off-shell

Particles of mass \(m\) can have a momentum whose invariant square does not satisfy the on-shell condition \(p^2=m^2\). These particle are unstable and will decay into on-shell particles. The further they are from the right mass the most likely it is that they will decay. The prime example is an off-shell photon produced in the anihilation of a electron-positron pair. Its invariant mass is \(s=(p_{e^-}+p_{e+})^2\) and the further away from the on-shell value \(0\) it is the least likely it is to be produced. This is seen in the fact that the anihilation cross section is decreasing as \(1/s\).

On-shell

Particles have given rest mass. If the invariant mass of the four vector representing their momentum is equal the the squared rest mass, then a particle is on-shell. The name comes from the surface in 4-dimensional space of possible momenta that satisfies the condition \(p^2=m^2\). This surface is called mass shell. In the case of a massless particle it is also called the light-cone.

Parity

Parity is a symmetry that reflects space, taking \(\vec x\) to \(-\vec x\). Since applying parity operator \(P\) twice takes us back to where we started \(P^2=1\) so parity eigenstates have eigenvalues \(1\) or \(-1\), for particles it is called the intrinsic parity. Parity is conserved by the strong and electromagnetic forces but is violated by the weak force. Parity is a multiplicative quantum number: the parity of a bound state is the product of the parities of the constituents of the bound state multiplied by the parity of the wave function that describes the bound state. If this wave function is written in terms of spherical harmonics the parity of the wave function is \((-1)^l\). Fermions and anti-ferminos have opposite intrinsic parities, so that bound states of a fermion and an anti-fermion (such as \(q\bar q\) in a meson) have negative intrinsic parity (it can become positive if they have an odd \(l\) spatial wave function).

Phase-space

The phase-space for a process is the set of all possible kinematical final states energies and momenta the particle in the final state can assume. It is normally the integral over all energies and momenta, subject to the energy-momentum conservation constraints. Let’s consider a few examples.

  • In the decay of a massive particle with spin \(0\) in its centre of mass system into two massless particles the energy of the daughter particles have to equal to half the mass of the decaying particle. The momentum has to be equal in magnitude to the energy and opposite so that the total momentum is equal to \(0\) (to match the momentum of the decaying particle). So the phase-space can be parametrised in this case with the direction of flight of one of the particles, all other quantities being fixed by the energy-momentum conservation constraints.

  • In the discussion of deep inelastic scattering we started with elastic scattering of the electron off the proton. In this case the phase-space has the same type of constraints than in the above example except that now not all particles are massless. We have two energies and momenta for the the electron and recoiled proton, that is 8 parameters. We have four constraints from momentum conservation, and two on-shell conditions, one for the electron \(p_e^2=m_e^2\) and one for the proton \(p_p^2=m_p^2\) so that we have two free parameters. One can be taken to be the azimuthal angle around the electron direction. Since this is a symmetry of the system the cross section will not depend on it so we only have one relevant quantity. It can be taken to be the scattering angle \(\Theta\) or the momentum transfer squared \(Q^2=-q^2\). When we go into the inelastic regime the last constraint on the momentum of the proton is released and we now have two independent quantities, which can be chosen to be either \(x,Q^2\), \(\nu,\Theta\).

Photon

The photon is the carrier of the electromagetic force. Its mass is \(0\). When it is on-shell, it cna travel long distances and be detected. As an off-shell particle it can be produced in the anihilation of a particle-antiparticle pair of charged particles.

Positron

The positron is the anti-particle of the lepton.

Pseudo-scalar

a pseudo-scalar object is an object that is a invariant under rotations but has negative parity, meaning its sign changes under a parity change. For particles this means \(J^P=0^-\). This is the case for spin \(0\) mesons.

Pseudo-vector

A pseudo-vector object is an object that rotates in the same way than a position vector under rotations but has a negative parity, meaning its sign does not changes under a parity transformation, which is the opposite behaviour as that of a“normal” vector. For particles this means \(J^P=1^+\). This is the case for the spin vector.

Qcd

Quantum Chromodynamics is the study of the strong force in quantum field theory. The strong force is acting between coloured particles, hence the “chromo” in the name.

Qed

Quantum Electrodynamics is the study of the electromagnetic force in quantum field theory.

Quark

Quarks are the constituents of the mesons and the baryons. They come in six flavours: up (\(u\)), charm (\(c\)), top (\(t\)) with charge \(+2/3\), down (\(d\)), strange (\(s\)) and bottom (\(b\)) with charge \(-1/3\).

Radiative_capture

Radiative capture is the process in which a nucleus absorbs a neutron, which brings it to an excited state and emits a photn to go to a more stable state.

Resonance

If we drive a mechanical oscillatory system of resonant frequency \(\Omega\) with an oscillatory force close to the resonant frequency we see that the amplitude of the oscillation grows and gets largest at the resonance frequency. In analogy to this system if we tune the centre of mass energy of a electron-positron close to the mass of an unstable particle the cross section will increase, reach a maximum at the mass of the resonance and decrease for centre of mass energies larger than the mass of the particle. A resonance is characterised by its mass and width.

Scalar

a scalar object is an object that is a invariant under rotations and has positive parity, meaning it is invariant under a parity transformation. For particles this means \(J^P=0^+\).

Singlet

A singlet is a multiplet with a single element. Singlets are invariant under the symmetry transformation that is used to define the multiplets.

Strangeness

Strangeness is an additive quantum number of the strange quark. A \(s\) quark has strangeness \(-1\) and a \(\bar s\) anti-quark ahs strangeness \(S=+1\). (One can remember that the sign of strangeness is the same as the sign of the electric charge). Strangeness is conserved by the strong and electromagtetic force but can be violated by the weak force.

Su2

\(SU(2)\) is the special group of unitary matrices. matrices \(U\) in this group have the property that \(U^+U=1\) (which makes then unitary) and have unit determinant. \(SU(2)\) is the group of transformations of two states that preserve the norm and overlap of states.

Su3

\(SU(3)\) is the special group of unitary matrices. matrices \(U\) in this group have the property that \(U^+U=1\) (which makes then unitary) and have unit determinant. \(SU(3)\) is the group of transformations of three states that preserve the norm and overlap of states. For example a rotation in the space of quark flavours \(u\), \(d\) and \(s\) (which does not affect the strong force) is an element of \(SU(3)\).

Tau

The tau is the heaviest lepton, It has the same quantum numbers as the electron or muon but is heavier. The tau can decay in a tau neutrino and either hadrons or another charged lepton.

Triplet

A triplet is a multiplet with three elements. A spin one particle is a member of a triplet with three states.

Unitary

A unitary matrix \(U\) is a matrix such that \(U^+U=UU^+=1\). Unitary matrices are important in quantum mechanics as they are the set of matrices that maintain the scalar product of states. Symmetry transformations are expressed as unitary transformations.

Vector

A pseudo-vector object is an object that rotates in the same way than a position vector under rotations. A vector has negative parity, meaning its sign changes under a parity transformation, which is the same behaviour as a position vector. For particles this means \(J^P=1^-\). This is the case for spin \(1\) mesons.

Width

The width of an unstable particle is given by \[\Gamma=\int\frac{(2\pi)^4}{2M}|\mathcal{M}|^2\;d\phi\] where \(\mathcal{M}\) is the matrix element for the process. The matrix element squared needs to be integrated over the phase-space for the decay to give the width. The width says how likely it is for a decay to proceed. The width is related to the lifetime \(\tau\) of a particle through \(\tau=1/\Gamma\). One can define partial width as the width for the particle to decay in a specific channel. The sum of all partial widths give the total width.
